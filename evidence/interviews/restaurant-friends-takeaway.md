## Key Findings

- Daily doner meat surplus is discarded every night at closing. This is a consistent, predictable waste stream.
- The business closes at 2am, creating a structural timing barrier. Most charities and volunteer drivers are unlikely to be available at that hour.
- The owner needs the app to work in under 3-5 minutes from start to finish. Any friction beyond that risks abandonment, especially during a busy shift.
- Wants a liability disclaimer clearly visible in the app. Concerned about responsibility if food causes illness after handover.
- Wants pre-saved allergen profiles so repeat donations do not require re-entering the same allergen information each time (e.g., doner meat always contains the same allergens).

## Direct Quotes

> "At the end of the night there's always meat left on the doner. We just throw it away. Every night."

> "Just come when you can, if it's 11pm or midnight, that's fine, I'll be here."

## What Changed in the App

This interview backed up the timing change (replacing the 4 datetime fields with a 2-tap availability selector). Tasty Kitchen and Friends Takeaway both said independently that the original datetime approach was too complex for real use. Late-night donors like Friends Takeaway are a forward strategy thing: the current MVP is built around standard operating hours, but nothing about the platform's architecture prevents us adding late-night collection windows later.

## Summary

Friends Takeaway is a typical donor profile: a fast-food business with predictable daily surplus and not much time to engage with an app. The interview backed up the timing simplification we already heard about from Tasty Kitchen and added two new things to think about: making liability clear somewhere in the app, and letting donors save allergen profiles so they don't have to re-enter them every day. The late-night hours are a segment we could serve, but it would need driver availability beyond typical volunteer hours.
