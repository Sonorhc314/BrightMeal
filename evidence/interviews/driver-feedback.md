# Volunteer Driver Feedback

| Detail | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Volunteer Driver Feedback |
| **Participants** | 7 (referenced as D1-D7) |
| **Method** | In-person prototype walkthrough |
| **Interviewer** | Riad |
| **Date** | 10 April 2026 |

## Key Findings

### Positives

- Job cards were easy to scan. Drivers could pick out the relevant info quickly and decide whether a job was for them.
- Storage banners (chilled, frozen, ambient) did their job. Drivers took storage requirements into account when choosing jobs, which is exactly what the banners are there for.
- The **Navigate to Pickup / Navigate to Delivery** buttons were the most praised feature across all 7 participants. One-tap nav to the collection or drop-off point was seen as essential.
- The delivery timeline was described as satisfying and easy to follow. Drivers could see where the donation was at any point without getting lost.
- 5 out of 7 said knowing which charity the food goes to mattered to them. Seeing the recipient made the job feel meaningful rather than abstract.

### Issues Identified

- 6 out of 7 asked what the points system means. There's no explanation anywhere of how they're earned, what they represent, or whether they unlock anything. That takes a lot of the motivation out of the gamification.
- Distance to pickup wasn't shown. Drivers had to guess from the address alone, which made it hard to judge whether a job was practical.
- No transport mode filter. Cyclists saw the same jobs as van drivers even though the capacity and range are totally different.
- 2 drivers were confused by the "Accepted" status label. They read it as "another driver already took this job" when it actually means a charity has accepted the donation and it's now available for a driver to pick up.

## Direct Quotes

> **D1:** "It feels like Deliveroo but for doing good. I'd use this on my way home from uni a few times a week."

> **D4:** "I already cycle past half these streets every day. If there's a job on my route, I'd stop and do it without thinking twice."

> **D5:** "Show me that what I'm doing actually matters: numbers, impact, the charity I helped. Then I'll keep coming back."

> **D6:** "Give me a leaderboard with just drivers on it and I'd be checking this every day."

## What Changed in the App

These findings feed into the forward strategy as prioritised improvements we'd make beyond the current MVP:

- **Transport mode filter**: let drivers set their transport type (bicycle, car, van) and filter jobs by what's feasible for their mode.
- **Distance display**: show estimated distance to pickup on the job cards so drivers aren't trying to work it out from the address.
- **Points explanation**: add some onboarding or tooltip content that explains how points are earned and what milestones exist.
- **Driver-specific leaderboard**: the current leaderboard shows all roles together. Drivers wanted a driver-only view so they can compete within their own community.

The positives from the sessions (job card scannability, storage banners, navigation buttons, timeline, charity visibility) didn't need any changes since they already work.

## Summary

The driver testing confirmed the core job browsing and delivery flow works. The nav buttons, storage banners, and timeline all held up well. The gaps are mostly informational (distance, points explanation) and structural (transport mode filtering), all noted as forward strategy. What motivates volunteer drivers most is seeing their impact (which charity they helped) and competitive elements like leaderboards, both of which the app already partially supports.
