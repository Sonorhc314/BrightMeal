# Usability Test Report

## Details

- **Conducted by:** Tori Rybalka
- **Date:** 17/04/2026
- **Participant:** Charity worker, no prior experience with food donation apps
- **Role tested:** Charity
- **Device:** iPhone 13
- **Duration:** ~20 minutes
- **Consent:** Participant gave verbal consent to the test being documented.

## Method

**Think-aloud protocol:** The participant was asked to say what she was thinking as she went through each task: what she was looking at, what she expected, and where she got confused. I only stepped in if she got completely stuck.

## Pre-Test Questions

1. Have you used any food donation, delivery, or volunteering apps before? No.
2. What would you expect an app like BrightMeal to let you do? "I expect to choose ingredients, to see pictures of how they look, so I know for a fact that everything is okay with them. I expect to have allergens listed, due dates, and for them to be reasonable and not overdue. I expect to communicate the exact time of reception with drivers or shops or restaurants since our schedule is tight."

## Tasks

1. Sign up and create a charity profile
2. Browse available offers and accept one
3. Check the leaderboard and badge progress
4. View donation history

## Observations

|---|------|-----------|-----------------|------|-------------------|

**General think-aloud:** "I like the colour palette, it doesn't strain the eyes. Seems like a nice and professional application. A lot of useful information on the main page, but not too overbearing."

## Direct Quotes

- "How do the developers know that we are a trusted charity? Is there a verification process?"
- "You can't decline an offer once you accept it. There should be an instruction for that."
- "Couldn't find the badge progress at first, cause I was looking for them on a 'Rank' page."
- "I've noticed there are no notes on the product (e.g. milk) brands or their due dates/photos — would be useful to see."
- "What is that heart in the corner? I can't click on it."
- "It would be nice to choose multiple options at once when looking for offers, e.g. bakery and dairy."

## Post-Test Questions

1. How easy was the app to use overall? (1 = very difficult, 5 = very easy) **2**: "overall easy to use, but some information seems to be missing" (comments were largely positive; numeric score reflects the information-gap findings).
2. What was the most confusing part? "No option to decline an offer when you've accepted it. But there is an option to decline before you accept — what is this for?"
3. What did you like the most? "The design has a nice palette that doesn't strain the eyes. I liked the logo and badges. The app works fast and is minimalist, which makes it easier to use."
4. Would you use an app like this? Why or why not? "Yes, it's a good app overall. There is some space for improvement, but I am sure it will be possible to do the mentioned fixes as soon as people start using it."

## Issues Found

1. **High**: Insufficient per-item detail in mixed-SKU donation listings (e.g. "12 pieces mix of dairy and oat milk" without per-type quantities).
2. **High**: No option to partially accept a donation (e.g. 5 of 14 items); all-or-nothing acceptance limits flexibility.
3. **High**: Missing product-level context for packaged items: brands, per-item expiry dates, photos.
4. **High**: No pre-acceptance communication channel with donor (no message/call before committing to accept).
5. **High**: Cannot undo/cancel an accepted offer from the charity side once accepted.
6. **Medium**: Charity sign-up accepts any email and has no verification mechanism (Charity Commission number, domain validation, or manual review).
7. **Medium**: Address input at sign-up is a single free-text field rather than structured (postcode, street, town, county) or autocomplete.
8. **Medium**: No multi-select category filter when browsing offers, can only filter by one category at a time.
9. **Medium**: Donor ratings shown without any written review/comment context.
10. **Medium**: Discoverability of badge progress is poor. Participant expected it on the Rank page, not the Profile page.
11. **Medium**: "Decline Offer" button pre-acceptance feels redundant; participant's mental model was "it's a listing, I just don't act on it".
12. **Medium**: Poor personalisation/matching of offers. Participant saw many cooked meals when her charity needed other categories.
13. **Low**: Required form fields lack visual indicators (e.g. asterisks).
14. **Low**: Decorative heart icon in the charity offers header looked clickable but wasn't, causing confusion.
15. **Low**: History page has no empty-state message or guidance when there are no completed donations yet.

## Suggestions from Participant

- Add more detailed/structured address fields or postcode-based autocomplete at sign-up.
- Introduce a charity verification step (official email domains or manual review against a registry).
- Mark required fields visually (asterisks or similar).
- Enable multi-select category filters when browsing offers.
- Show written reviews/comments alongside donor ratings.
- Break down mixed donations by sub-quantity (e.g. "8 × dairy + 4 × oat milk").
- Allow partial acceptance of a donation.
- Capture richer product info at posting time: brand, per-item expiry, photos.
- Provide a message/call option before acceptance, not only after.
- Allow charities to decline/undo an accepted offer with a clear flow.
- Move badge progress to a more discoverable location (Rank page or a visible entry point).
- Improve filtering/personalisation so charities see relevant offer categories first.
- Add an empty-state message to the history page.

## Reflections

The participant was really engaged, this being her first time with an app like this, and I got a lot out of the session. Three things stood out:

The first was around trust. She wanted to know how charities get verified on the platform. Leo's participant raised the exact same thing, so now two charity testers have flagged it independently, which makes it a strong signal.

The second was about what charities see before they accept an offer. She wanted partial acceptance, per-item breakdowns, brand and expiry and photos for each item, and the ability to message the donor before committing. Leo didn't surface this, but it fits with what the charities in our interviews kept saying: they want to be sure about the food before they commit to picking it up.

The third was the decline and cancel flow. She thought the pre-acceptance decline button was pointless (it's a listing, just ignore it) and wanted a way to cancel an offer after accepting. Leo's participant hit the same thing from a different angle, where the decline button didn't actually remove the offer. The current decline/cancel setup clearly doesn't match how charities think about it.

On the positive side, the design, palette, speed, and minimalism got good comments across the board, which is consistent with what every charity participant has said. The 2/5 score with positive comments is worth flagging, since Leo's participant did the same thing, where the number looks harsh but the commentary is warm. Most of her suggestions are scope for a future version rather than things we could ship now. Partial acceptance and pre-accept messaging especially would be major chunks of work, so they're documented as forward strategy.
